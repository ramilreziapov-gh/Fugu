Runaway Star.

( A Faint Red Star Might Leave The Milky Way.)

A faint red star was discovered, moving about 1.3 million miles per hour (600 kilometers per second). This rare stellar speedster is the first "hypervelocity" very low mass star found,
located just 400 light-years from Earth, it is the nearest known hypervelocity star to us.

This star may be on an unusual trajectory that could cause it to leave the Milky Way altogether.
The orbit is certainly the most surprising aspect of this object; it is moving radially in and out of the center of the Milky Way and almost perfectly in the plane.
The star was named CWISE J124909+362116.0 (or "J1249+36" for short).

What gave this star a kick?

Researchers focused on two possible scenarios to explain J1249+36's unusual trajectory.

In the first scenario, J1249+36 was originally the low-mass companion of a white dwarf. In one of the types of white dwarfs, if the white dwarf collects too much mass, it can collapse and explode as a supernova.
In this kind of supernova, the white dwarf is completely destroyed, so its companion is released and flies off at whatever orbital speed it was originally moving, plus a little bit of a kick from the supernova explosion as well.

In the second scenario, J1249+36 was originally a member of a globular cluster, a tightly bound cluster of stars, immediately recognizable by its distinct spherical shape.
The centers of these clusters are predicted to contain black holes of a wide range of masses.
These black holes can also form binaries, and such systems turn out to be great catapults for any stars that happen to wander too close to them.
